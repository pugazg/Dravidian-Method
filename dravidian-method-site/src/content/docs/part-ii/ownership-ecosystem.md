> “Ownership did not become less important. It became harder to see.”

## A New Kind of Concentration

For much of the twentieth century, the central questions of development were straightforward.

Who receives education?

Who gains representation?

Who gets employed?

Who enters public institutions?

These were the questions that shaped the great expansion of opportunity across Tamil Nadu.

They were institutional questions.

Governments could answer them with schools, universities, reservations, scholarships and public employment.

But success altered the landscape.

A society may succeed in widening participation while still concentrating creation.

People may enter industries without owning them.

They may work inside innovation without directing it.

They may produce knowledge without controlling the institutions through which knowledge becomes wealth.

The question therefore changes.

Not who participates.

Who creates?

Who owns?

---

## Ownership Changed Its Form

Anna examined mills.

Insurance companies.

Banks.

Managing agencies.

Industrial houses.

These were the visible concentrations of his age.

Today’s economy looks different.

Some of the world’s most valuable firms own few factories.

Their greatest assets are software.

Algorithms.

Patents.

Design.

Research.

Data.

Brand.

Networks.

Relationships.

The physical economy has not disappeared.

But increasing economic value now emerges from knowledge itself.

Ownership has become increasingly intangible.

The concentration remains.

The object has changed.

---

## Knowledge Alone Is Not Ownership

Modern societies often celebrate education as the solution to economic inequality.

Education is indispensable.

But education alone does not automatically create ownership.

A brilliant engineer employed by a multinational corporation possesses extraordinary knowledge.

Yet the patents belong elsewhere.

The platform belongs elsewhere.

The investors belong elsewhere.

The company belongs elsewhere.

Knowledge can generate value without generating ownership.

That distinction explains why educational success does not automatically produce entrepreneurial success.

The pathway between them contains another ingredient.

---

## The Missing Ingredient

That ingredient is the ecosystem.

Ideas require researchers.

Researchers require laboratories.

Laboratories require funding.

Funding requires investors.

Investors require confidence.

Confidence grows through relationships.

Relationships accumulate through repeated interaction.

Customers create markets.

Successful firms create experienced founders.

Experienced founders create mentors.

Mentors become investors.

Investors support new founders.

Knowledge begins circulating rather than merely existing.

Capital begins circulating rather than merely accumulating.

The system starts reproducing itself.

That is an ecosystem.

---

## Institutions And Ecosystems

Institutions and ecosystems are often confused.

They are not the same.

A university is an institution.

An ecosystem forms when universities, entrepreneurs, investors, corporations, researchers and government agencies repeatedly interact over long periods of time.

An incubator is an institution.

An ecosystem appears when companies leaving that incubator continue helping future companies.

A venture fund is an institution.

An ecosystem emerges when successful founders become the next generation of investors.

Institutions are components.

Ecosystems are relationships.

The distinction matters because relationships cannot simply be announced.

They must develop.

---

## Why Ecosystems Produce Ownership

Ownership rarely appears as an isolated event.

Most founders do not begin with complete knowledge.

They borrow experience.

They inherit advice.

They receive introductions.

They meet investors through existing founders.

They recruit former colleagues.

They collaborate with researchers.

Their customers recommend them.

Every successful entrepreneur therefore inherits an invisible infrastructure created by previous generations.

Ownership becomes cumulative.

Not because success is predetermined.

But because ecosystems reduce uncertainty.

They lower the cost of beginning.

They accelerate learning.

They multiply opportunity.

This explains why some regions repeatedly produce entrepreneurs while others struggle despite having equally talented people.

Talent alone is rarely enough.

The surrounding ecosystem matters.

---

## Ecosystems Reproduce Themselves

The most remarkable feature of an ecosystem is not that it creates success.

It is that success begins creating more success.

Employees leave successful companies to establish new ones.

Founders become investors.

Researchers become entrepreneurs.

Customers become partners.

Universities become anchors.

Each generation becomes part of the environment in which the next generation grows.

Outputs become inputs.

The system acquires memory.

That is why mature ecosystems appear almost self-sustaining.

Their greatest resource is no longer money.

It is accumulated relationships.

---

## The State Encounters A New Problem

This creates an unfamiliar challenge for democratic governments.

Schools can be constructed.

Scholarships can be expanded.

Hospitals can be financed.

Roads can be built.

These are provisionable goods.

Relationships are different.

Governments cannot legislate trust.

They cannot command mentorship.

They cannot order investors to believe in an entrepreneur.

They cannot decree that founders will help future founders.

The state therefore encounters a resource it cannot directly administer.

It can encourage.

It can convene.

It can support.

It can reduce friction.

It can create opportunities for interaction.

But the ecosystem itself must eventually become self-reproducing.

---

## A Different Language Begins To Appear

This helps explain an interesting shift in development policy.

Earlier generations spoke primarily about education, employment and industrialisation.

Contemporary policy increasingly speaks about innovation.

Commercialisation.

Technology transfer.

Research parks.

Startup ecosystems.

Angel networks.

Venture capital.

Deep Tech.

Innovation districts.

The vocabulary has changed because the object has changed.

Governments are no longer concerned only with producing skilled workers.

Increasingly they seek to cultivate environments in which skilled workers become founders, researchers become innovators and ideas become enterprises.

The language of ecosystems reflects a recognition that ownership grows differently from access.

---

## The Next Question

Recognising the importance of ecosystems, however, does not answer a more difficult question.

Can governments deliberately cultivate them?

Can relationships be accelerated?

Can networks be widened?

Can ownership itself become more broadly distributed?

Or do ecosystems inevitably reproduce existing concentrations of capital, trust and opportunity?

These questions move us beyond theory.

They require evidence.

Fortunately, Tamil Nadu has spent the past several years conducting one of the most interesting experiments in ecosystem-building anywhere in India.

It is to that experiment that we now turn.
